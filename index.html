Sinai… Land of Tribes and Sacredness

Sinai is not just a place on the map — it is a living testament to history, an open stage for great civilizations, and home to tribes that have preserved the identity of the land for thousands of years.

Ancient Egyptians, Hyksos, Nabataeans, and Romans left inscriptions, temples, and trade routes that still echo through the land.

Sinai… Land of Tribes and Sacredness

From the dawn of humanity, Sinai has witnessed early settlements...

With the Islamic conquest, Arab tribes entered Sinai and made it their home...

In the rugged mountains, powerful tribes like Al-Qarārsha and Awlad Saeed have carved lives amidst stone and spirit. Their resilience echoes through every pass and path.

In the southern valleys, tribes like Al-Muzaynah, Al-Aleyqat, Al-Hamādah continue to preserve heritage through ancient customs and oral storytelling.

Women in Sinai, especially in the highlands, are the heart of everyday life. Their craftsmanship, culture, and courage form the backbone of tribal continuity.

The Bedouins of Sinai are not just residents — they are the soul of the desert, the carriers of oral traditions, and the custodians of sacred landscapes.

Geographically, Sinai is a rare mosaic of towering peaks, deep wadis, and spiritual silence.

🕍 Saint Catherine’s Monastery

Where sacred history lives at the foot of Mount Sinai.

🕍 Introduction

Founded in the 6th century, this sacred site marks where Moses saw the Burning Bush. Saint Catherine’s Monastery stands as a living symbol of spiritual resilience and divine encounter.

🧙‍♂️ Life of the Monks

The monks live in silence and prayer, cultivating gardens, transcribing ancient texts, and embracing lives of reflection and simplicity under the Sinai sky.

📚 Monastery Library

  • 6,000+ manuscripts (Greek, Syriac, Arabic, Coptic…)
  • Codex Sinaiticus – the oldest complete Bible
  • 2nd only to the Vatican in sacred texts

🕌 The Fatimid Mosque

A 10th-century mosque nestled inside the Christian monastery — a rare example of harmony and coexistence, with its wooden minbar and mihrab still intact.

🌿 Burning Bush & Moses’ Well

A living thorn bush believed to be the Burning Bush still grows near Moses’ Well — a sacred connection to the divine.

📝 Prophet Muhammad’s Covenant ﷺ

  • Guaranteed safety & religious freedom
  • Exemption from military duty & taxation
  • A legacy of mutual respect and tolerance

Famous Mountains of Sinai

From sacred summits to geological wonders, discover the iconic mountains that shape the soul of Sinai.

  • 2,285 m Mount Sinai (Jebel Musa) Believed to be where Moses received the Ten Commandments. Known for its spiritual significance and sunrise view.
  • 2,070 m Mount Serbal Composed of red granite, this mountain is steeped in Christian monastic history and mystery.
  • 2,030 m Bab el Dunya Named “The Gate of the World”, it features a natural opening with breathtaking views of the Red Sea.
  • 2,642 m Mount Saint Catherine The highest point in Egypt. Named after Saint Catherine of Alexandria, it offers panoramic views and hosts a small chapel and a weather station.
  • 2,586 m Mount Umm Shomer Remote and rugged, this hidden gem is Egypt’s second-highest peak and a challenge for serious hikers.
  • 2,383 m Mount Abbas Basha Built by Khedive Abbas I for health reasons, its ruins overlook dramatic valleys and surrounding peaks.
  • 2,029 m Mount Tarboush Named after the fez-like shape of its peak, offering scenic treks and colorful rock formations.
  • ~2,000 m Mount Safsafa A quiet retreat near the monastery, known for its solitude and early Christian hermitages.
  • 1,763 m Jebel el Banat Named after a legend of fleeing girls, this mountain blends folklore with gentle hiking trails.
  • ~1,700 m Mount Naaja Part of the Bedouin hiking network, Mount Naaja offers peaceful paths with stunning views over Wadi El Arbein.
  • Jebel Matamir A surreal sandstone mountain range of elongated hills, towering dunes, and secluded basins. Starting near the Nawamis site, treks can extend to Jebel Makharum and Haduda dune. Its signature is a maze of elevated sandy basins surrounded by jagged peaks.
  • Wadi Al-Barqa, near Nuweiba El Makhroom Mountain Known as “Dinosaur Mountain” for the hole that pierces through it, this limestone wonder offers the thrilling “Eye of the Dinosaur” climb. Surrounded by dunes and caves, it's a gateway to Sinai’s ancient geology and raw beauty.

Wadi Al-Talaa

Wadi Al-Talaa is a serene mountain valley near Mount Catherine. It is known for its tranquility and its proximity to historical religious sites including an ancient monastery believed to have hosted pilgrims and monks. Surrounded by jagged peaks and desert silence, it’s a haven for spiritual hikes and cultural exploration.

Wadi Ittlah

Wadi Ittlah houses the Monastery of St. John Climacus and is known for its winding trails, serene caves, and ancient steps carved by monks and wanderers. It’s a perfect blend of spiritual heritage and geological marvels.

Wadi Al-Arbaeen

Wadi Al-Arbaeen is steeped in myth and biblical references. It runs parallel to the ancient path taken by prophets, marked by large boulders and whispering winds. Its silence tells stories older than memory.

Wadi Gharbah

Known for its wild herbs and ancient Bedouin settlements, Wadi Gharbah is a living museum. The valley carries stories of healing, survival, and Bedouin resilience beneath its sandstone cliffs.

Wadi Ghazala

Named after the gazelles that once roamed freely, Wadi Ghazala is known for its reddish hues and surreal sunset views. It’s a favorite for desert camping and stargazing.

Wadi Maktab

Wadi Maktab, the “Valley of the Writing,” is home to Nabatean inscriptions and rock art. Every cliff is a script, every echo a tale from caravans and forgotten poets.

Wadi Jebal

Surrounded by towering mountain walls, Wadi Jebal is a dramatic and rugged path that attracts trekkers seeking raw nature and sacred silence. It connects to the ancient pilgrim routes.

Colored Canyon

The Colored Canyon is a kaleidoscope of layered sandstone—red, yellow, and purple. Its narrow passageways and vibrant walls make it one of Sinai’s most photographed sites.

White Canyon

With its chalky white walls, the White Canyon feels like walking through ice in the desert. It’s a hidden treasure near Ain Khudra oasis, offering silence and surreal beauty.

Salama Canyon

Salama Canyon, less known but equally enchanting, winds through golden cliffs and narrow turns. It is cherished by local guides for its peaceful atmosphere and dramatic views.
